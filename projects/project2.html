Automated Star - Galaxy classification with KNN!

Astronomical imaging is a powerful tool for capturing celestial objects along the line of sight direction. However, when studying external galaxies, the resulting image contains not only the stars within the galaxy of interest but also background galaxies. This presents a challenge for researchers, as determining the distances to individual stellar objects is often difficult. As a result, classification based on image analysis alone is necessary.

Sources within astronomical images can be classified into two broad categories: point sources (such as stars) and extended sources (such as background galaxies). Traditionally, photometric diagnostic parameters such as error, sharpness, and crowding parameters have been used to classify these sources. However, the classification accuracy of these methods is not always optimal, and there is often no definitive answer for classification.

To improve the accuracy of source classification, researchers have recently explored the use of machine learning algorithms. One such algorithm is the K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), which has shown promising results in classifying sources in astronomical images. KNN works by training the algorithm on a large dataset of known sources with known classifications. The algorithm then uses this training dataset to classify sources in new images. KNN's ability to handle complex relationships between parameters and its simplicity make it an attractive option for source classification in astronomical images.

We utilized the Hubble eXtreme Deep Field (HXDF), which is a deep field observed with the Hubble Space Telescope (HST), to train the KNN algorithm. This field was chosen for its unique location in the sky, which is devoid of any nearby galaxies and bright stars, making all the sources in this field background galaxies (flag=0). This deep field provides an excellent dataset for training the KNN algorithm, as all the sources in this field can be considered as background galaxies.

To make a list of point sources (flag=1) in the training set, we performed artificial star tests. Artificial stars with known point spread function (PSF) models were injected into the image, and we recovered them in the same way as the real objects in the HXDF were detected. By doing this, we created a training sample for point and extended sources, along with their photometric diagnostic parameters, such as error, sharpness, roundness, and crowding. These parameters were used to train the KNN classifier to identify and classify the sources in the HXDF accurately.

This approach enabled us to classify sources in the ESO410-G005 data accurately, providing valuable into the properties and characteristics of the sources in this field. By using the HXDF as a training sample, we were able to create a reliable and accurate KNN classifier that could classify sources in other fields with similar characteristics. Overall, our study demonstrates the effectiveness of the KNN algorithm for classifying astronomical sources and highlights the importance of creating a good training sample to achieve accurate classification results (Jang et al. in preparation).
