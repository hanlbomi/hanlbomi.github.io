Linear Regression

Linear regression is a fundamental machine learning algorithm used for modeling the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. It assumes a linear relationship, where the dependent variable can be predicted by finding the best-fit line through the data points. The algorithm aims to minimize the difference between the predicted values and the actual values to make accurate predictions.

Forecasting house prices in Chicago
Kaggle[1] offers a range of valuable datasets suitable for data science projects. The dataset utilized in this case is sourced from Kaggle and specifically focuses on house prices in the suburbs of Chicago. It comprises 157 examples with nine variables, which are structured as columns.

  • Price : price of house (scaled)
  • Bedroom: number of bedrooms
  • Room: number of rooms
  • Space : size of house (in square feet)
  • Lot : width of a lot
  • Tax : amount of annual tax
  • Bathroom : number of bathrooms
  • Garage : number of garage
  • Condition: condition of house (1 if good , 0 otherwise)

A histogram is a straightforward data visualization method that provides an overview of the global distribution of a variable. For instance, by examining the histogram for the "Price" variable, we can observe that it ranges from 30 to 90, with an approximate mean value around 55. The presence of a longer tail on the right side of the histogram suggests that the price distribution is not Gaussian and contains outliers, representing expensive houses.
While histograms are intuitive and provide a quick understanding of the data, they do have certain limitations. For example, 1) histograms may require careful selection of bin sizes to accurately represent the underlying distribution, and 2) they can become visually crowded when comparing multiple distributions.

A box plot serves as an alternative visualization that provides a succinct summary of essential statistics, including the median, quartiles, and potential outliers. It facilitates straightforward comparisons among multiple distributions and offers a reliable visual assessment of the data's central tendency, spread, and outlier presence. The box plot comprises four key features:

  • Median (Q2): The line inside the box represents the dataset's median, indicating the central tendency.

  • Interquartile Range (IQR): The box represents the interquartile range (IQR), which is the range between the 25th percentile (Q1) and the 75th percentile (Q3). It encompasses the middle 50% of the data.

  • Whiskers: The whiskers extend from the box and indicate the data range, excluding any outliers. The lower whisker extends to the minimum value within 1.5 times the IQR below the 25th percentile (Q1), and the upper whisker extends to the maximum value within 1.5 times the IQR above the 75th percentile (Q3).

  • Outliers: Individual data points that fall beyond the whiskers are considered outliers and are represented as individual points.

Referring back to the price plot, it is evident that approximately half of the data falls within the 45-65 range, and there are no prominent outliers deviating from the overall data distribution.

Our objective is to develop a machine learning (ML) model for predicting Chicago house prices based on variables such as Bedroom, Space, Lot, etc. It is important to understand the underlying data properties before applying ML linear regression models. Regression is not a unique solution but rather approximates the global data distribution, so comprehending the data's distribution in the relevant domain is crucial. The figure above illustrates the distribution of individual variables as a function of Price. It reveals a notable linear relationship between Price and variables like Space, Room, Tax, Bathroom, and Garage. However, the relationship is less apparent for Bedroom, Lot, and Condition, indicating a potentially weaker association that the ML model may capture.

To develop a robust machine learning (ML) model, it is advisable to follow a step-by-step approach. One important step involves conducting correlation analysis among the columns in the DataFrame using the "Pearson" method. In the provided figure, the diagonal terms are expected to have a value of 1 as they represent the correlation of each variable with itself. Examining the "Price" variable, we observe a strong positive correlation with variables such as Space, Room, Tax, Bathroom, and Garage (e.g., Corr > 0.5), which further validates the findings from the visual inspection.

In order to construct our predictive model, we employ the "LinearRegression" module from the scikit-learn package. The dataset is carefully divided into two distinct groups: a training set, which serves as the foundation for model development, and a separate testing set, which allows us to evaluate the model's performance and generalization capabilities. It is worth noting that the test dataset comprises 48 samples, making up roughly 30% of the overall sample size. By applying a straightforward linear regression model to the training set, we derive the coefficients showcased in the left table.


The obtained model coefficients allow us to approximate the house price using the following linear relation:

Price = -2.85 × Bedroom + 0.01 × Space + 2.42 × Room + 0.16 × Lot + 0.01 × Tax + 4.48 × Bathroom + 3.72 × Garage + 16.90

I addtion to the basic fit parameters, we obtain several evaluation metrics, such as absolute error (MAE), mean squared error (MSE), root mean squared error (RMSE), and model score.

  • Mean Absolute Error (MAE) : Measures the average absolute difference between predicted and actual values. Lower values indicate closer predictions.

  • Mean Squared Error (MSE): Measures the average squared difference between predicted and actual values. Lower values indicate lower overall error.

  • Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) : Square root of MSE, providing an interpretable metric in the original scale.

  • Model Score : Represents the coefficient of determination (R-squared), indicating the proportion of variance in house prices explained by the independent variables. Higher scores indicate better predictive power.

The trained model is then applied to the test dataset, yielding a Model Score value of 0.61, indicating a reasonably good fit. Finally, we anticipate the price of a typical house in the Chicago suburb area with three bedrooms, 1600 square feet of space, 1.5 bathrooms, and 1 garage, assuming other parameters. The model predicts a price of 58.11 (scaled house price).
