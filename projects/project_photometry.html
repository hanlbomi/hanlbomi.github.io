Resolved Stellar Populations

Resolved stellar populations, the study of individual stars within a galaxy or a cluster, provide a wealth of information about the formation and evolution of galaxies, as well as the properties and evolution of individual stars. By analyzing the physical properties of resolved stars such as their positions, colors, luminosities, and chemical abundances, astronomers can gain insights into the processes that led to the formation and evolution of galaxies and their constituent stars.


One of the key advantages of studying resolved stellar populations is the ability to analyze the properties of stars of different ages within a given population. This allows astronomers to study the evolution of galaxies over cosmic time and the mechanisms that drive their formation and evolution. For example, observations of globular clusters have revealed multiple generations of stars with different ages and chemical compositions, providing insights into the early history of the universe and the processes that led to the formation of these clusters. Similarly, observations of open clusters have provided information about the formation and evolution of stars in the Milky Way's disk over the past few billion years.

The Carnegie Chicago Hubble Program (CCHP) is a collaborative effort between the Carnegie Observatories, the University of Chicago, and the Space Telescope Science Institute. The program's primary objective is to improve the accuracy of the Hubble constant, a fundamental value in astronomy that describes the rate at which the universe is expanding. To achieve this goal, the CCHP is using several standard candles, including a specific type of variable star called Cepheid variables, and the Tip of the Red Giant Branch (TRGB) to measure cosmic distances.
Cepheid variables pulsate at regular intervals, and their brightness is directly related to their intrinsic luminosity, making them useful standard candles for determining cosmic distances. However, Cepheids are limited to relatively nearby galaxies, and their use becomes increasingly uncertain at larger distances. To extend the distance scale beyond the range of Cepheids, the CCHP employs the TRGB, a feature in the brightness of red giant stars that marks a well-defined luminosity. The TRGB is an excellent standard candle that can be used to measure distances to galaxies out to several tens of millions of light-years, providing crucial information for determining the Hubble constant with high precision.
The CCHP's observations of Cepheid variables and the TRGB, combined with measurements of the cosmic microwave background radiation, have refined our understanding of the universe's age and size and have important implications for theories of dark energy, dark matter, and the ultimate fate of the universe. In addition to its scientific goals, the CCHP also has an outreach component that engages with the public and encourages students and educators to learn more about astronomy and the methods used to study the cosmos.
